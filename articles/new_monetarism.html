Reading the Error Message

Four weeks ago, the American people went to the polls and voted to remove the Biden administration, returning Donald Trump to the Oval Office and the position of Commander in Chief. This has been the most consequential and upsetting election result of my lifetime.

In the wake of this disaster, the atmosphere feels markedly different from that of 2016. Back then, fiery reactions of resistance and horror erupted from the liberal masses. Now, however, I'm seeing a transformation within the liberal intelligentsia‚Äîa shift toward depressive self-doubt. Vibrant democratic thought withdraws into itself amidst it's defeat, healthily conceding it's rejection by the American people in favor of the MAGA movement. There is an introspective effort to grapple with the immense question of how to craft a constructive narrative of the American dream that can reconcile the failures leading to this man's reelection. We are witnessing sparks of a long-overdue intellectual attempt to locate and dissolve the source behind liberal America's inability to identify and address this repudiation of Bidenomics and the Democratic coalition's agenda in its entirety, largely forced out by the democratic will.

But I would like to discuss those questions a bit.

Amidst this tension, I feel that some of the strongest arguments explaining why the Democrats were so thoroughly rejected boil down to issues of macroeconomic reality. These arguments generally proceed as follows: A whipsaw of society reopening after COVID-19, brittle globalized supply chains, and built-up consumer savings gluts caused trillions of dollars to flood into a restricted supply pool. This surge led to inflation spiking and price levels increasing by some 20 percent since 2020. Such unprecedented rises in price levels have inspired fury among voters across the board in one of the most election-dense years in human history. Universally, these voters have elected to throw out their ruling parties, pointing the finger at them for real pain caused by these economic conditions.

There are important aspects that this narrative overlooks. The media environment and the failure to conduct a proper presidential primary process are, in my mind, the primary missing pieces. Nonetheless, I think the overall narrative is correct‚Äîto a point.

However, I don't believe this is solely about COVID-19. I think the pandemic was the catalyst that revealed a cost-of-living crisis arising from an intersection of failures within liberal institutions that have been left unaddressed for quite some time now. The veneer of a decade-and-a-half-long, globalization-induced zero-inflation landscape has been cast off. The American people are seeing the direction in which society is moving in liberal America and have recognized the need for change. I happen to agree, and I believe there are some compelling reasons to do so.

Liberal America is failing to understand economic reality, and I felt it would be an interesting exercise to explore not only the mainstream theories of the economy but also some of the more iconoclastic schools that have been gaining traction outside the halls of academia.

Stepping Through...

The very first theory of economics was Adam Smith's school of Classical Economics, also known as laissez-faire economics. Under this framework, markets were seen as self-regulating through the invisible hand of self-interest, and government was advised to take as minimal a role in the economy as possible.

Classical Economics dominated academic thought up until the Great Depression, which seemed to prove that free markets could, in fact, bring about truly deep and prolonged dysfunction. Dynamics of extended mass unemployment and fears of economic stagnation began to boil over. In response, the ideas of Keynesianism rose alongside the guns of a big New Deal American government, which endorsed and seemed to validate its ideas.

Under the Keynesian School of Economics, it was argued that the government should be the rational actor to smooth over the highs and lows of business cycles. This meant supporting active government intervention in times of economic breakdown and government saving in times of sustained economic growth. Keynesians didn't necessarily argue against the long-term wisdom of the invisible hand of the free market, but they rebuked it with the now-famous assertion that "in the long run, we are all dead." They contended that the government cannot let people live in squalor today for the sake of a not obviously forthcoming well-off future. Short-run failures of the market needed to be righted to put the economy back on track in the short term.

The government, as an economic actor itself, could step in when aggregate demand collapsed. As the sole holder of rights to print money, it could create money to spend in the economy and bring economic activity back up to healthy levels. Keynesianism aligned itself with a revision of Classical Economics known as Neoclassical Economics, which incorporated new theoretical progress in rational choice theory. This highlighted the importance of government in controlling economic agents' expectations through policy posturing, which would influence the state of things going forward and encourage differing views of the future that impact current behavior. Together, Keynesian and Neoclassical tools of supply and demand formed what is known as the Neoclassical Synthesis.

Unfortunately, come the 1970s, in an America with the Great Depression far in its rearview mirror, a new economic syndrome began to show its face‚Äîone which Keynesianism proved unable to address. This spawned yet another new era of economic thought: Monetarism, as well as a splinter faction in the form of the Austrian School of Economics.

Going into the 1970s, the U.S. government was already stimulating the economy heavily due to poor rates of productivity increases. Then, OPEC‚Äîthe cartel of oil-producing countries‚Äîembargoed the United States, causing inflation to skyrocket. An inflationary cycle took place where workers demanded higher wages to keep up with inflation, and in return, businesses passed the increased labor costs onto consumers, creating a self-reinforcing spiral. Economic growth was low, and inflation was high, directly challenging Keynesianism's call for stimulus in times of inflation.

To address this, the new Monetarist school rose with a theory centered around the supply of money and an increased importance of the Federal Reserve in regulating the national economy. Under the Monetarist framework, the money supply was the unifying pool into which all levers of government fed to control the economy. The Federal Reserve, as the sole institution empowered to print U.S. dollars, became the most important economic actor in town‚Äîbeyond Congress, which could force the Fed to print money for government spending.

Monetarists maintained the theory of economic cycles from Keynesianism and built upon it, arguing that inflation is the product of the supply of money growing faster than the bucket of real economic output being purchased with that money. They described inflation mathematically under the formula:

\( MV=PQ \), where \(M\) is the money supply, \(V\) is the velocity of money, \(P\) is the price level, and \(Q\) is real output. Monetarists argued that the Federal Reserve could reduce inflation through tools explicitly designed to decrease the amount of money flowing through the economy.

A natural rate of unemployment was defined, as well as a correlation between the money supply and the inflation consumers were experiencing. A new, strict Federal Reserve chairmanship began under Paul Volcker, who subscribed to this new Monetarist school. He spiked interest rates to cool down economic activity, causing not only the recession of the early 1980s but also the successful curtailment of the inflation spiral that the school had risen to address.

Simultaneously, guided by desires to counteract the Cold War tendencies toward central planning‚Äîwhich existed in the USSR at the time and were seen by many in the Monetarist school‚Äîthe Austrian School of Economics grew. It described this activist role of the Federal Reserve as the source of economic cycles of booms and busts in the first place. The Austrian School took issue with the entire project of Monetarism by arguing that its attempt to treat the money supply as something controllable by government policy was an inherently flawed departure from the tie that currency should have to the physical commodities to which they had historically been pegged (such as the gold standard).

Austrian Economics supported deregulation of the banking sector and minimalist monetary intervention, describing business cycles as natural phenomena where government intervention only serves to prolong economic problems that represent the working through of market adjustments.

This combined into the neoliberal equilibrium of Supply-Side Economics. While the Federal Reserve would manage monetary policy‚Äîthe supply of money‚Äîthe rest of the government was handling fiscal policy. Arguments began to dominate that economic activity was being stymied by excessive taxation. Taxes were slashed‚Äîas well as the social policies they funded‚Äîin favor of beliefs that businesses would use the extra money to invest more in boosting economic output. A case in point is the infamous Laffer Curve.

This is the point at which the modern rejections of economics as a field in its entirety, which I see everywhere around me‚Äîboth at my university and among my generation as a whole‚ÄîI believe owe their roots. But it wasn't until the financial crisis of 2008 that these misunderstandings truly came to light.

The financial crisis of 2008 highlighted that there was more to economic collapse than mismanagement of the supply of money. Supply-Side Economics had deregulated its way into a situation where a few systemically important banks took on excessive internal leverage, engaged in risky lending, and indulged in greed-fueled speculation to manufacture imaginary bubbles of economic growth detached from reality and bound to eventually pop. Economic thought began to understand that certain private organizations had grown to become "too big to fail" and that neither the Monetarist school had the conceptual tools to tackle the shortfalls of financial mismanagement nor could the Austrian method of letting the free market fail be allowed to drag the global economy into its greatest crisis since the Great Depression.

Out of this grew entirely new fields of economics. One was Behavioral Economics, which analyzed the psychological dynamics behind how humans act irrationally and how that translates into unhealthy market equilibriums and financial bubbles. Another was Post-Keynesian Theory, which built on Hyman Minsky's Financial Instability Hypothesis to describe how stability breeds instability through increased risk-taking in financial markets. This emphasized the role of financial markets in influencing the supply of money as the source of credit within the economy, essentially providing a synthesis of the Monetarist and Austrian schools of thought.

Post-Keynesianism subsumed Monetarism as the theoretical grounding for its endogenous money supply while articulating new tools for measuring the financial industry as a source of an exogenous money multiplier. In the decade and a half after 2008, during which I was growing up, we saw a process of globalization that caused wage growth to slow and prices to fall as supply chains became more efficient. This efficiency was achieved by utilizing global competitive advantages and economies of scale alongside digitalization, which reduced costs across the economy through efficiencies and better information for consumer goods. Aging demographics also slowed consumer spending.

This translated into job loss and extremely low levels of inflation, which the Federal Reserve managed through a 15-year regime of near-zero interest rates. It wasn't until the storm of reopening after COVID-19‚Äîwhere globalized supply chains proved far more difficult to logistically restart and trillions flowed back into the market‚Äîthat the Federal Reserve actually had to pivot to controlling inflation. While experts would tell you that they have largely done a good job at this‚Äîwith inflation back down and unemployment actually below what Monetarist underpinnings tell us it should be‚Äîpeople had also forgotten what it feels like to see inflation and responded with fury.

Debugging üêû

Much like in the 1980s and 2008, I see two main new schools of heterodox economic thought that have been evolving under the surface of the current establishment of economic theory. I don't necessarily agree with these theories‚Äîthe first one I'll introduce I'm even fairly sure is very wrong‚Äîbut I think they're both interesting as a way to dig into some of the freshest ground where issues are being raised with the current way of doing economic thought.

The first theory I would mention is Modern Monetary Theory (MMT). While Austrian Economics critiqued the Monetarist school by arguing against the idea of governments' ability to institute fiat currency‚Äîclaiming that the value of money is based on the physical commodity to which it is tied‚ÄîModern Monetary Theory builds on the chartalist view of money. Chartalism describes the value of money as arising not from some pegging to a physical commodity of specific value but instead from the exclusive right of the government to levy taxes on economic activity. This legitimizes that country's currency as the exclusive medium for valuing all of the economic activity within its borders.

Chartalism shows its fingerprints in much of early economics, from Adam Smith and Jean-Baptiste Say to John Stuart Mill and even Karl Marx. Today, MMT has risen back to popularity largely as a justification for raising taxation to fund government expansion.

Modern Monetary Theory claims that the primary path to full employment is through fiscal policy rather than monetary policy, which is the mainstream narrative. It describes the federal government as breathing value into its currency, where fiscal spending is limited solely by inflation that happens when real resources are being fully utilized‚Äîthat is, near full employment. MMT rose to prominence in the modern era through shout-outs by politicians like Bernie Sanders and Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez. It has played a role in delegitimizing fears of taking on debt denominated in one's own government's currency, promoting fiscal spending, and pushing for active federal programs to encourage or directly provide employment.

The primary critique of MMT lies in its overemphasis of the power of the sovereign over its own currency‚Äîa point any foreign exchange trader will readily discuss. Not only do the values of currencies move based on demand for that currency in international transactions or because of central bank rate arbitrage opportunities, but borrowers largely depend on the stable value of currency in order to loan in that currency. Anti-MMT critiques argue that undercutting this dynamic threatens credit issuance across the economy, which dampens overall economic activity and investment.

Another critique of MMT is that it may be better to put non-elected officials in charge of handling macroeconomic fluctuations rather than elected politicians, who may be encouraged to overheat the economy to win short-term electoral victories.

This brings me to the second heterodox economic theory, about which I will be writing much more and which I think is very much worth discussing: Neo-Monetarist Economics.

Neo-Monetarism grows out of the finance crowd and the economic ideas of Fisher Black‚Äîone of the creators of the Black-Scholes model for financial markets containing derivative investment instruments‚Äîand Eugene Fama, an expert on modern portfolio management theory. Much like how Post-Keynesianism incorporated financial markets as an exogenous supply outside the direct endogenous M2 money supply, Neo-Monetarism claims that the "supply of money" needs to be reimagined. It envisions the economy as fundamentally built on stocks of wealth‚Äîdifferently liquid assets being traded around with, often, money as the intermediate step of transaction.

In the eyes of Neo-Monetarists, while money is useful, the core driver of the growth of stocks of wealth and the amount of credit being issued from those stocks is the liquidity of a huge swath of asset classes with varying returns. Thus, the supply of money becomes some measure of the size of the total assets being traded, adjusted for the liquidity of each of those assets. Debt and equity become not just additions and subtractions from the size of the economy but, as beautifully shown in the >Modigliani‚ÄìMiller theorem, two differently structured classes of capital to value economic enterprises by.

In this view, while the old theory of Monetarism based on cash supply used to be quite correct, today liquidity is jointly produced by the Federal Reserve and the private market‚Äîwith the private market mattering more and more as the field of financial instruments grows.

Thus the growth rate of the economy becomes much harder to model as it depends on the exercising of non-uniform embedded options markets, although some starter concepts within Fed circles on how to account for this difference discuss reformulating the growth rate formula from being expressed in terms of the sheer sum of M2 money supply sources [1]: \[ \Delta M_t = \sum_{j=1}^{n} x_{jt} - \sum_{j=1}^{n} x_{j(t-1)} \]

To the weighted sum of all of the different asset classes being traded across the economy (weighted by liquidity aka usefulness as a medium of exchange): \[ \Delta \log M_t^D = \sum_{j=1}^n s_{jt}^* \left( \log x_{jt} - \log x_{j,t-1} \right), \] where each asset class is weighted according to it's share of expenditures averaged over two periods of change: \[ s_{jt}^* = \frac{1}{2} \left( s_{jt} + s_{j,t-1} \right), \quad s_{jt} = \frac{\pi_{jt} x_{jt}}{\sum_{k=1}^n \pi_{kt} x_{kt}}, \] and \[ \pi_{jt} = \frac{R_t - r_{jt}}{1 + R_t}, \] with \(R_t\) as the benchmark yield and \(r_{jt}\) the yield of the \(j\)th asset. Thus making \(\pi_{jt} \) the opportunity cost of holding a unit's worth of the \(r_{jt}\)th asset.

The core problem being pointed to here is that it's becoming more and more difficult to identify what indeed even is the "money supply." A house, for example, is pretty illiquid, but there's a chance you will borrow against your house, meaning that there's some factor of liquid credit correlating to that asset besides the sheer value of selling it. It's quite an open and technically difficult problem to solve, but the point is to say that currency is not nearly as influential as an asset pool in influencing the behavior of the market compared to other asset classes. In fact, most of the supply of physical cash isn't even inside the United States, meaning that the behavior of cash in particular may depend more on behavior in foreign economies or even just in illicit markets like the drug trade than on other more core asset classes, which perhaps represent the lever of the American asset stock allocation and growth much more.

Some levers could still be said to determine the money supply‚Äîmostly expectations of future economic behavior based on business conditions, history, and the Federal Reserve's posturing‚Äîbut also things like private liquidity and international relations.

The hope is that by redefining the supply of money to be expansive, we could then tease out a clearer signal of why it has been so hard to statistically observe the relationship between the Fed's impression of the money supply and the actual effects of their policies on economic behavior. Factors like the success of the post-2008 Federal Reserve change to providing an interest rate on bank reserves‚Äîaway from an explicit required amount of reserves‚Äîdrive the amount of bank reserves from billions into trillions overnight. This shows the economy ought to be viewed through the lens of assets rather than hard currency being passed around between traders.

One key policy takeaway of Neo-Monetarism is that the Federal Reserve is actually far more underpowered than people currently understand it to be. The role of banks as the sole source of lending by the Federal Reserve needs to be abandoned. Viewed through the lens of who is providing liquidity in the market, banks shrink to only around 20 percent of the whole asset stock, and the rest of the economy‚Äîlike the insurance industry, where the crisis of 2008 really started‚Äîbecomes far more important.

Another implication is that the case for alternative monies and the rise of asset classes like cryptocurrencies becomes not only far more predictable but almost inevitable. The market begins to find other ways to process transactions more suitable for exchanging different asset classes, especially across foreign boundaries, which compose the majority of its mass. Digital coins might become more useful to handle as the medium of transactions than physical currency. How could the medium of account not, at a bare minimum, be seen as some complex intersection of global currencies, given the impact of multinational corporations‚Äîwhich operate using dozens of currencies at once‚Äîon nearly every locality on Earth? Under this light, the U.S. stands to gain the most from the proliferation of cryptocurrency around the world, as it allows actors around the globe to more quickly move from their local exchanges into USD-denominated accounts.

Moreover, given the redefinition of what constitutes the supply of money, perhaps the structure of theoretically predicted expected behavior by economic agents more closely mimics the perception of economic hardship that consumers have been feeling for years. This hardship led, in large part, to the Democrats' rebuttal but which the current structure of Monetarist economic thought fails to encapsulate in its outdated measurements of what provides the bread and butter of everyday economic asset stocks for the American people. Could this help to provide the previously undefined phantom source for the so-called "Vibecession"?

Financial assets are the money of the modern era, and Neo-Monetarism describes the challenge of measuring that new complex of differently structured assets as the key source of noise to control for in bringing the predictions of classic Monetarism into the 21st century.

Recompiling üèãÔ∏è

I did not invent the concept of the cost-of-living crisis, but I do consider myself the first person yet to connect the idea of the cost-of-living crisis to the Vibecession and to the source of misunderstandings of the structure of economic hardship. These misunderstandings are misguiding policy and misguiding the calculations of progressive, forward-thinking policymakers today.

Experts will tell you that the Biden administration guided the U.S. into a position of global leadership in economic growth and inflation curtailment. As many have pointed out, that very well may have registered in the economy, leading to the defeat of the Democrats receiving the unenviable award of "smallest rejection" of the year. But given the fundamentally different structure of expectation propagation that Neo-Monetarism implies‚Äîrather than the far more outdated model we're largely thinking within‚Äîwe begin to see the importance of the crisis in home construction rates and austerity-inspired individual fiscal tightening downstream from the dissolution of the welfare state as more important than ever previously considered in determining what people look to as the economic indicators that matter to them.

The primary critique of monetary norms that I've seen coming out of this is that people really hate inflation, and the Federal Reserve needs to be even more strict in curtailing inflation next time around. Small amounts of inflation are hugely more electorally destabilizing than a few ticks of unemployment. I think this is true, but I also think it highlights that the indices being measured to indicate the direction of the money supply and velocity within the economy are deeply flawed‚Äîblind to the realities of the financialized system which Americans actually rely on for the credit and asset accumulation that any and everyone would tell you is the lifeblood of building personal financial success in the 21st century.

So, what is to be done?

We need to build new houses, grease the lines of credit which the American people rely on to make the type of long-term asset accumulation they depend upon, and cut red tape to invest in the capital-intensive factories that fuel financially cutting-edge industrial models like those of the tech industry today.

The aperture of results we need to be measuring within the economy to declare a job well done needs to be widened to encompass all of the various capital classes that make someone really feel like they're living in the vibrant society of a global superpower. Otherwise, we will continue to see the American people throw us out of office in favor of the person telling them that the experts don't know what they're talking about and to give him sole power to "burn it all down and build, baby, build."

This is the vision I see of a productive narrative for a new American Dream, and a significant portion of my answer to the question: What did we get wrong?
