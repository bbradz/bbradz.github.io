The Census finds that of the 140 million homes in the US around 95 million (or about 67.8%) are single-family. 3/4 of all residential land in American cities is zoned exclusively for detached single-family homes with the same rate holding for the entire state of California .

Why Car-based Suburbia Sucks:

Important Note: I won‚Äôt be touching on redlining b/c it‚Äôs external to the economic perspective: optimizing growth w/ minimal effect on the environment to grow the pie as much, and as sustainably, as possible. Before we built our communities around the expectation of every family having access to a car to get around they reflected a certain natural and healthy balance. Homes and businesses were built close enough together that even small town‚Äôs downtowns would have everything you ever needed within a convenient walking distance in exchange for a higher cost of living. Alternatively, those living further away from downtown had worse access (although still being within walking distance, because people still needed to buy food) but could afford more space in a town‚Äôs lower-demand periphery. The size of towns and how far people could live from them was inherently constrained by how far someone could reasonably be expected to walk which was not that far, ergo, promoting density.

But with the introduction of cars, people and businesses were suddenly able to move out of traditional town centers onto cheap plots of previously rural/unoccupied land. Economically, individual agents no longer have that financial incentive to stay compact since as long as a road exists connecting residential areas to commercial areas, it takes comparatively much longer distances until commute issues to being to matter. This incentivized the abandonment of urban centers in favor of building spread-out neighborhoods and strip-malls on previously cheap land, in the process making driving a necessity to get around and causing societal changes that can be seen everywhere.

Stepping back; The Broken Economics of Suburbia:

Essentially every single issue associated with Suburbia stems from one Economic phenomenon, a phenomenon which we‚Äôve left untreated and allowed to fester and grow for nearly 80 years now: Externalities

In Economics, Externalities are costs caused by an actor making a decision where some of the negative effects of that decision aren‚Äôt financially felt by the actor for making that decision (aka Having your cake but not having to Eat it too).

There are 3 major Externalities associated with Urban Sprawl:

  1. The Cheapness of Ecosystem Destruction: Developers aren‚Äôt taxed for the Ecosystems which they end out paving over and destroying by building new houses. In fact, the low cost of unoccupied land even goes as far as to incentivize the building of new developments, encouraging building outward instead of upward.

  1. The Cheapness of Greenhouse Gas Emissions: While there are Gas Taxes which are used to fund road maintenance of a bit under 50¬¢ per gallon on average depending on the state, not only have these taxes been flat (aka falling because of inflation) since 1993, they‚Äôre nowhere near that of countries like Germany, Italy, and the UK where gas taxes are nearly x6 higher (~$2.50-$3 per gallon). We‚Äôve got absolutely NO Carbon Taxes, NO Energy Taxes, NO Nitrogen Oxide Taxes and all of it adds up to mean that there‚Äôs not only less money collected to maintain roads than in other those other countries, but that individuals and companies are given blank checks to drive around, running up their emissions and hurting everyone.

  2. And, finally, The Cheapness to Degrade the Roads we drive on: Why do you think we have to maintain those roads in the first place? Sure, some of it is environmental but cars play an enormous part in it as well and drivers aren‚Äôt held accountable for it at all. Oh, and DON‚ÄôT come at me telling me that Gas Taxes are supposed to be covering that as well, do they help? Of course. But much more important in determining Road Degradation than distance driven is the weight of the vehicle being driven.

    Let me introduce you to a little known relationship known as the Generalized Fourth Power Law. The 4th Power Law is the product of the 1961 AASHO Road Test and determined that damage caused by vehicles is tied to the 4th power of their axle weight. Meaning that a 8,600 lbs Hummer (2,150 lbs per axle), in comparison to a 4 axle Prius weighing 3,050 lbs (750 lbs per axle), since it weighs roughly 2.87 times more, causes comparatively 2.87^4 (aka 67.85 times) as much road degradation.

    For a huge amount of reasons (which I don‚Äôt have the time or motivation to write about here) America, unlike Europe, is the home of superheavy Pickup Trucks and SUVs like the top-selling Ford F-150‚Äôs and Rav F-4‚Äôs, and while the average European car weighs 3,086 pounds, the average American one weighs nearly 1.32 times as much, a whopping 4,079 pounds. That alone means that American cars cause 1.32^4 or 3.04 times as much road degradation as their European alternatives.

    • Note: There‚Äôs a broader point to be made here on how car transit, from a physics perspective just fucking sucks in comparison to other forms of transport like (in descending order of efficiency): maglev, trains, trams, and buses. But that‚Äôs something for another article because its not really about Housing policy.

Economists know how to solve Externalities, you just need government action which forces the cost of those Externalities on to the people choosing to cause them, aka the trigger-word of 10s of millions of Americans: Taxes.

The Externalities, produced by public policy failures, which have undergirded and fueled the building of Suburbia are destroying our economy, environment, and ultimately society. The time has come to put an end to this madness.

A backbreaking undersupply of Housing

In order to shift away from Suburban life towards restoring our high-density Urban cores to their past glory we need to take a look at some of the issues in those Urban cores which are causing American cities to be forced to build those Suburbs in the first place. A line of questioning which essentially universally leads back to the irrefutable fact that cities are refusing to build the new housing needed to accommodate the American public.

There‚Äôs a shortage of homes from coast to coast the scale of which we‚Äôve never seen before in the United States, especially concentrated in cities like New York, SF, Boston, Miami, DC, LA, Seattle, and San Jose a housing shortage which is spilling over and spreading into more and more auxiliary cities with every passing month.

Putting some numbers to that, the Public Policy Institute of California analyzed data from the 2020 US Census and found that over the last 10 years California added 3.2 times more people than it did housing units, causing there to currently be 2.93 Californians for every occupied housing unit. And although coastal housing is the most expensive, the biggest percent change in housing undersupply has actually occurred in inland regions where, to avoid higher coastal prices, many people moved to the Central Valley, east of the Bay Area, and the Inland Empire east of Los Angeles. In the process, they have driven up prices in their new neighborhoods as well.

The Legislative Analyst‚Äôs Office found in 2015 that over a 30-year period from 1980 to 2010 the state needed to build at least 70,000 more units each year and perhaps as many as 110,000. Adding up to a shortfall of about 3.5 million units.

The Impact of Cities‚Äô Housing Undersupply

Not only are the Suburbs regionally surrounding these major cities seeing demand skyrocket in order to accommodate those willing to commute in to those urban cores but entire cities like Fresno, Modesto, Austin, and Houston are seeing housing shortages of their own as those previously mentioned megacities export those who can‚Äôt afford to live in them to other cheaper cities around the country, knock-on leading to the housing markets of smaller cities becoming overwhelmed as well and the exporting the housing shortage to those cities as well.

Far right column shows the price increase over the course of 2021

Rent is soaring. The price of homes in SF is higher now, even adjusted for inflation, than it was at the peak of the dot com bubble causing the city to get so prohibitively expensive at this point that even someone making $100K per year could now find it difficult to pay for the cost of living. It‚Äôs to the point where not only are our countries largest cities seeing >30% rises in rent YOY but that 11 million households, or 1 in 4 renters, are spending more than half of their monthly income on their rent‚Ä¶ although experts say that figure is likely even higher now (Harvard Joint Center for Housing Studies).

Even worse, those who traditionally lived in these cities before these housing shortages who can‚Äôt afford the soaring costs of living are increasingly finding it impossible to put a roof over their heads, leading to nearly 580,000 Homeless Americans nationwide concentrated on the streets of these major cities and major gentrification of their once culturally vibrant and deep-knit communities.

But let‚Äôs for the sake of argument throw that all out the window and disregard how this housing shortage is hurting the lives of 10s of millions of Americans particularly those who it leaves on the street to die and the disproportionately minority communities which it breaks apart. We would still need to understand that this housing shortage is holding back the entire US and wider world (yes, through promoting Suburbia but also‚Ä¶) by driving a spear into the heart of the innovative clusters of talent that produce the inventions, ideas, and ultimately growth which we as a country rely on in order to thrive and prosper.

We Need Strong Cities.

Academics of all types from Economists to Urbanists all love to talk about the importance of the clustered networks of individuals and firms working and living together in a shared environment which generates the tech clusters we see in major cities like SF, NY, Boston, Seattle, and LA. The reason being that, all else equal, (as a matter of 1,000s of years worth of empirical fact) having more smart people interacting as much as possible leads to more ideas which leads to more innovation which leads to more growth.

These agglomeration effects as Economists call them occur in cities all across the world and are really the fundamental reason why things like Cities, Universities , and Corporate HQs exist in the first place. One tremendous advantage which the US enjoys is the unique geopolitical advantage of having the premier industrial clusters for essential technologies like software development and machine learning which will shape the paths of billions of lives and trillions of dollars worth of economic activity for decades to come.

In the 1800s, Lowell, Massachusetts was the center for textile mills relying on water power. By the early 1900s, Cleveland was pushing forward the frontier of electricity and steel. Later Detroit and Michigan, of course, developed into the powerhouse for automobile manufacturing in the mid-1900s. And now, American tech clusters are the envy of the world. Of the four Trillion-dollar companies in the world, two are based near San Francisco (Apple and Alphabet), and two near Seattle (Amazon and Microsoft) with 14 of the top 30 Internet firms being based in SF alone.

The effects that tech clusters have on innovation and productivity growth are extremely impressive and well understood, even on the individual level with studies finding that: ‚Äúa computer scientist moving from a median-sized cluster in CS like Gainesville, FL to a 75th percentile sized-cluster in Richmond, VA experience a 12.0% increase in productivity. This trend continues outside of CS too with workers a move by someone working in biology and chemistry from a median cluster‚ÄîBoise, ID‚Äîto a 75th percentile cluster‚ÄîState College, PA‚Äî being associated with a productivity gain of 8.4%; and as clusters get larger and more specialized, the productivity boosts also get larger.

Larger and larger tech clusters lead to disproportionately larger gains in economic activity, high-skilled technical employment levels, the number of patents granted, and R&D funding to bankroll innovation. They‚Äôre were the most interesting conversations and cutting-edge applications in a particular field happen.

Which, if anything, shows even more why the housing crisis in these countries is so alarming. Because of that housing shortage, up-and-coming innovators are forced to pay 3x the national average in rent in order to live in places like Silicon Valley or Manhattan.

The True Cost‚Ä¶

Those aggregate costs of the misallocation of labor being pushed away from tech clusters by the high costs of living, means that workforce size hasn‚Äôt been able to grow in tandem with its productivity causing fewer workers to benefit from that increased productivity and preventing both individual cities and the country as a whole from maximizing economic potential. A 2009 joint study out of the University of Berkeley and University of Chicago places the aggregate cost of that lower worker-productivity at having slashed US GDP Growth between 1964 to 2009 by50%, costed the economy nearly $2 trillion in 2009 alone, and having cost the average US worker over $9,000 in lost income.

TLDR; It‚Äôs driving up rents. It‚Äôs driving up homeless rates. It‚Äôs gutting disproportionately-minority previously vibrant communities. It‚Äôs driving down productivity. It‚Äôs costing us trillions.**

How to fix it:

I. Build more housing! Studies show that building literally any type of housing from luxury to market-rate to affordable housing brings down the cost of rent for everyone from the richest to the poorest residents because increasing supply of housing, *surprise surprise*, brings down the cost of housing.

II. Reform local housing regulations!

  • Abolish Single-family Zoning which artificially limit supply, or better yet reform the zoning the American zoning code away from one of implicit restriction where you need to fight for new legal usages to one of default inclusion (like Japan) where we start from the presumption of all land uses being okay and scale back uses as needed.
  • Abolish parking minimums which force lots to spend more time accommodating cars and less time accommodating the people meant to live on them.
  • Abolish minimum lot size restrictions which subsidize large truck ownership and car ownership more generally at the expense of everyone else.
  • Abolish the discretionary review process for multi-family housing which keeps any new housing developments stuck behind so much red tape that they never see the light of day

III. Better yet, shift land-use decisions from the local level to the federal or state level! Housing is managed at a national level in the UK, Japan, and Singapore largely because the interest of the politically involved residents of a neighborhood (who, by the way, are almost exclusively homeowners who stand to profit from restricted housing supply and higher home prices) often isn‚Äôt in the interests of the general public.

[+] Addendum : Why We Can‚Äôt Build

March 29th, 2023

This entire post could be, curtly, summarized as: complaining about why too many low density places are being built and too little high density places are being built. One of the things I really tried to underscore throughout the article was the concrete policy failures which could be righted to help fight and reverse the status-quo we‚Äôve built ourselves into today. Well, I think I‚Äôve got a couple semi-different critiques worth mentioning that warrant this addendum.

I generally focused a lot of my effort in the above pages on explaining the fiscal policy failings causing these density issues, and I‚Äôm relatively happy with the detail I gave to that side of things. However: fiscal policy isn‚Äôt everything and in this past 1 year and 1 month I‚Äôve picked up another new policy to point to as a, I believe, important brick in the wall preventing America from unlocking its true potential for productive genuine density.

Turning to the field of regulatory policy, I would point a heavy hand at NEPA (the National Environmental Policy Act) as a massive piece in the puzzle of understanding why this country is so simply unable to build.

I am really not the type of person to take issue with regulations usually, one of the most important tasks government is uniquely saddled with is the regulation of society to protect everyone from private sector abuses which will hurt all of us. However, NEPA is different and it‚Äôs actually a really interesting deep dive to go about figuring exactly why.

To explain it overly quickly, the core flaw of NEPA is that it introduces too many veto powers for individuals to halt developments in their tracks at extremely low costs for extremely long periods of time, effectively delaying projects until the will dies.

In practice, NEPA is a 70s era monster responsible for regulating how the government is required to evaluate the environmental impacts of the projects it launches before it‚Äôs properly allowed to break ground and begin construction. NEPA outlines 3 different levels of review: categorical exclusion (easiest), an environmental assessment (mid), and an environmental impact statement (hardest).

NEPA enforcement is done through the courts with political action groups going to judges (who are often very willing to entertain their concerns out of a sense of misplaced caution) with nearly any complaint about the government having overlooked any of the environmental effects of their projects being able to stop all construction indefinitely until the concerns brought up are fully researched and alleviated if found.

While this, in theory, sounds like an okay system the issue comes from the vague wording of the law around what ‚Äúenvironmental effects‚Äù legally means and a vibes-based set of legal customs and precedents which have made NEPA easier and easier to abuse over time. NEPA gives anyone with a lawyer the power to nullify the plans for any project if they can just repeatedly find new and decreasingly relevant nitpicking issues which that project simply ~may~ with more research end up being capable of causing.

From Berkeley landowners delaying the building of housing by describing college students themselves as environmentally dangerous to SF‚Äôs $1.7 million public toilet (at the small scale), 3.5-7.2 year delays for Forest Fire Services to 5+ years and 8000+ pages of research delaying Seattle‚Äôs light rail expansion (a project crucial for reducing overall emissions from transportation‚Ä¶) the same story keeps happening.

A $2.6 billion offshore wind project close to my home, in Cape Cod, was cancelled after nearly 16 years of legal dogfighting over effects to the local fish populations and a $3 billion wind project in Wyoming took 11 years to finish review because of NEPA related legal quarrels.

We need to build in order to more sustainably restructure the physical layout of our nations neighborhoods. We need new clean energy infrastructure. We need modern infrastructure fit for a modern world and modern economic needs. NEPA locks all of those behind the need for near perfect unison on every possible implication of a project before it can become, and multiplied across the entire country that translates into a chilling effect on American‚Äôs understanding of what we can actually get done with the resources we have if we just allow them to go to work for us on real projects with real tangible benefits instead of decade+ review processes.

This is just an addendum, so I‚Äôm not going to go into extreme detail about this policy but let it be said here that another very important policy that I would push for redesigning would be the overly restrictive NEPA regime locking away developments all around the country.
